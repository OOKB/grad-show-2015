We invite you to join the next generation of creative thinkers—artists, designers, educators, curators and critics—as they demonstrate the power and possibilities of contemporary art and design.

MICA Grad Show 2015 includes exhibitions and critiques, gallery talks and presentations, public programs, a symposium, and student-curated installations throughout Baltimore city.

Join us for a season of innovative and inspiring events and exhibitions featuring the culminating work of more than 150 of MICA’s graduate students from the College’s internationally renowned programs.

  • Community Corners

  • Projection / Community Art
  • 2014

People frequently ask me to define what medium I work in as an artist: “Are you a painter? A sculptor?”

A couple of years ago this would have been easy to answer as I worked primarily in graphite-based conceptual art. But since my art has evolved into a socially engaged practice, I find that traditional mediums and descriptors do not capture the essence of my work. Coming from a background in transformative community organizing, much of my art is informed by working with groups of people to collectively mobilize around pressing issues that are shared in a community. I am inspired by artists such as Ai Weiwei, Kara Walker, Kimsooja, and Xu Bing who use their art to make arresting statements about politics and. society Similarly inspiring artists Rirkrit Tiravanija and Rick Lowe create meaningful spaces of dialogue, connection, and possibility between diverse groups of people. To me, art making is a social and political endeavor.

The grassroots and collaborative nature of my art requires that the process carry equal weight to the final product. My process involves many hours spent engaging in dialogue, interviews, and workshops with residents, students, business owners, and passersby from the neighborhoods in which I live and work. I create spaces for people to tell their own narratives and weave social autobiographies with others that translate local epistemologies and common bonds and knowledge cultivated through shared racialized, classed, nationalized, and gendered experiences into visual works.

Food is often incorporated in my work. As a Mixed Race, Asian American Woman of Color, I appreciate food not only for its power to bring people together through eating, buying, selling, or cooking with, for, or from each other, but also for the stories of violence, access, cultural heritage, trade, and commodity that are embedded within food. Food is an important component in my art making and community organizing. It is a prominent theme in my work and my life.

In a recent project I carried out in the fall of 2014 entitled Community Corners, I brought together residents and corner store owners of West Baltimore, neighborhood leaders, and artists to participate in a public dialogue about food access and health while viewing art and eating healthy dishes. Food was a part of the conceptual-political content, and it was also a community-building medium. The process of the work involved organizing and interviewing residents about their thoughts on the food situation along Pennsylvania Avenue, a historically Black arts and entertainment district in West Baltimore. The quotes that I collected from residents were displayed as a primary focal point in Community Corners (2014). Participants and viewers were provoked to reimagine corner stores as sites of connection, health, and sustainability instead of as sites of urban decay, drug abuse, and tense race relations. Corner stores historically and contemporarily have functioned as an integral component to neighborhood life. Sometimes they have been used as meeting places, sometimes as places to acquire nourishment, and sometimes as points of conflict. This project also functioned critically as a movement building action to draw attention to the work around health and food disparity being done by an active social justice organization in the neighborhood. Community Corners (2014) marked the beginning of my thesis work, Humble Presence, to be displayed this spring, 2015.

Whether the art I produce exists physically as an art object, such as a collaborative, youth-driven mural or whether the art has an ephemeral presence, like the projected installation and happening Community Corners (2014), cultivating relationships with people to build stronger neighborhoods, create spaces of empowerment, and elevate the voices, needs, wants, talents, and assets of the people involved is at the crux of my work.
