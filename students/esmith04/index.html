We invite you to join the next generation of creative thinkers—artists, designers, educators, curators and critics—as they demonstrate the power and possibilities of contemporary art and design.

MICA Grad Show 2015 includes exhibitions and critiques, gallery talks and presentations, public programs, a symposium, and student-curated installations throughout Baltimore city.

Join us for a season of innovative and inspiring events and exhibitions featuring the culminating work of more than 150 of MICA’s graduate students from the College’s internationally renowned programs.

  • Still from the Movie "Pi"

  • Pencil on board
  • 12in x 12in x in, 2014

This statement has been written from memory. Now that I have made that assertion, it is implied that these words may simultaneously be understood as an artist statement and a game with objective rules, where I recite the previous version correctly or incorrectly. Within the game beautiful and ugly things can happen. Outside of it, questions about the rules and decisions made can be asked. My work is an attempt to orchestrate these two approaches into a language I can use to communicate to others.

I have memorized pi to 10,000 digits, and recite it from memory in my work. The recitation has taken a myriad of forms, the most conventional being written language. I have also encoded information in the thickness, color, and shape of materials. Languages of technology serve to make other aspects of the recitation a part of the work. For instance, using digital programming to record and mimic my recitations of pi allows the rhythm of my recitation to become part of the work.

Ten cell phones vibrating one at a time in mimesis of my recitation of pi give an ordinary experience added meaning. It is neither a tribute nor a dismissal of technology, but a system where some of the things we hold most dear – our ability to memorize and recite, the mathematical constants we believe are objective – are viewed in the same way as anything else in life, such as technology. In this way the different levels are made commensurable. The reified recitation becomes an actor in the work, the same way that the other physical elements do, and has its own interests in relation to them. The subject of memorization is implied, and may be said to have this relationship or not.

Memorizing and reciting complex relationships such as random numbers is for me a highly meditative act. It is at once otiose (being random and abstract) and universal. The nature of this effort turns memorization of a subject into an act of quiet generosity towards it, as is recitation for an audience. This is similar to the way that I approach Tom Friedman’s 1,000 Hours of Staring, in which the artist stared at a piece of paper for the specified number of hours. Acts of kindness such as these ultimately form the personal impetus of my work.
